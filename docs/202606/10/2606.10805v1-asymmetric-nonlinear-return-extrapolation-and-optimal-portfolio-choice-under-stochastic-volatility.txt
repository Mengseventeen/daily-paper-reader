Dong Yan a, Wenrui Ye a, Zhiyue Zong a, Wenting Chen b∗

a. School of Statistics, University of International Business and Economics, Beijing, China. b. Department of Business, Jiangnan University, Wuxi, Jiangsu, China Wuxi, China. 

Abstract 

We extend the return extrapolation framework of Atmaz (2022) to incorporate two behav-iorally realistic features absent from the linear benchmark: saturation in belief updating and asymmetry between gains and losses. We introduce a smooth, nonlinear, asymmetric extrapolation function and characterize the optimal portfolio of a CRRA investor under Heston (1993) stochastic volatility as the sum of a sentiment-distorted myopic demand, a variance hedging demand, and a sentiment hedging demand.The resulting semilinear Hamil-ton–Jacobi–Bellman equation is solved by two independent numerical methods, a finite-difference ADI scheme with time-step policy iteration and a deep learning-driven iterative scheme. The model generates four investor-level behavioral anomalies: asymmetric responses to gains and losses, attenuated reactions at extremes, excess trading volume, and welfare Loss rising with the strength of extrapolation, each of which maps onto documented empirical patterns. Its central finding is that saturation acts as an endogenous correction mechanism: at the same local slope at the origin, the asymmetric nonlinear extrapolator carries a smaller welfare loss than a linear one. 

Keywords: Return extrapolation; behavioral portfolio choice; loss aversion; Weber–Fechner saturation; stochastic volatility; physics-informed neural networks.

# 1 Introduction 

How investors form expectations about future stock returns is one of the central open questions in modern asset pricing. A by-now substantial body of survey evidence indicates that, contrary to the rational-expectations benchmark, investors form expectations by extrapolating past returns: optimism rises after good performance and pessimism rises after bad performance, often at odds with subsequent realized returns (Vissing-Jorgensen, 2003; Greenwood and Shleifer, 2014; Cassella and Gulen, 2018). This pattern, commonly referred to as return extrapolation , has spawned a sizable theoretical literature aimed at understanding its asset-pricing and portfolio implications. Early contributions in this literature focus on equilibrium asset pricing under extrapola-tive beliefs. Barberis et al. (2015) and Barberis et al. (2018) embed extrapolators alongside fundamental traders in a tractable representative-agent framework and show that the result-ing equilibrium reproduces several stylized facts of return predictability and bubble dynamics. Adam et al. (2017) derive similar implications in a setting in which a Bayesian investor extrap-olates because of subjective-belief misspecification. Jin and Sui (2022) and Li and Liu (2023) extend the framework to cross-sectional implications and intermediated markets, respectively. Closely related to our work, Atmaz (2022) incorporates the linear extrapolative bias θX t

into a continuous-time stochastic-volatility economy (Heston, 1993). This framework yields closed-form solutions for equilibrium stock and option prices, demonstrating that return extrap-olation endogenously biases volatility expectations. Consequently, their model reconciles several puzzling empirical findings regarding the variance risk premium. Despite the empirical successes of the extant literature, two structural assumptions have remained largely unexamined.The first is linearity : belief distortion is modeled as a linear func-tion of past returns. A linear rule forces the investor to react to a given change in Xt by the same amount whether the current state is calm or extreme. The second is symmetry : gains and losses of equal magnitude generate equal but opposite distortions in beliefs. Both assumptions sit uncomfortably with well-established findings in behavioral economics. The psychophysical literature, going back to Fechner (1860), establishes that perceptual and cognitive responses to a stimulus exhibit saturation : the marginal sensitivity of perception decreases as the stimulus grows in magnitude, implying that investors should display attenuated reactions to extreme past returns. The prospect-theory literature (Kahneman and Tversky, 1979; Tversky and Kahneman, 1992) documents pervasive loss aversion : agents respond more strongly to losses than to gains of equal magnitude, with the asymmetric reaction estimated at a factor of approximately 2.25 .Direct experimental evidence corroborates this asymmetry in the context of return expectations specifically: Kuhnen and Knutson (2011) and Kuhnen (2015) show that subjects revise their return forecasts downward more aggressively after losses than upward after gains. Neither sat-uration nor asymmetry can be captured by a linear extrapolation rule, and to our knowledge no theoretical work to date has examined how these features alter the portfolio implications of extrapolation. This paper fills that gap. We develop a continuous-time portfolio-choice model that incor-porates both features by replacing the linear extrapolation θX t with a smooth, monotonic, and asymmetric mapping f (Xt). This mapping exhibits tail saturation governed by the parameter 2λ, and features a stronger response in the loss domain than in the gain domain, controlled by the asymmetry parameter β. The mapping nests the linear specification of Atmaz (2022) as a limiting case, ensuring that our framework is a strict generalization rather than a substitute. Building directly on the price dynamics of Atmaz (2022), we embed the nonlinear belief struc-ture in a partial-equilibrium portfolio-choice problem for a CRRA investor and characterize the resulting optimal dynamic strategy. We make three contributions, one analytical, one methodological, and one conceptual. The analytical core is a closed-form decomposition of the optimal portfolio. Under nonlinear ex-trapolation, the CRRA investor’s weight is the sum of a sentiment-distorted myopic demand, a variance hedging demand, and a sentiment hedging demand. From this decomposition we obtain three comparative statics, each matching a pattern documented in household and behav-ioral finance: the portfolio reacts more strongly to losses than to equal-sized gains, its marginal sensitivity to sentiment flattens out in extreme states, and the welfare loss relative to a rational investor rises with the strength of extrapolation. The attenuation and welfare results are signed analytically. The asymmetry is signed in closed form for the myopic component and confirmed numerically once the hedging demands are added. The model also speaks to excess trading vol-ume, but only in part: the sentiment-driven channel is signed, while the sign of total turnover is left to the numerical analysis.Methodologically, we solve the semilinear HJB equation with two complementary methods. The first is a finite-difference ADI scheme with policy iteration nested inside each time step. The second is a Physics-Informed Neural Network with policy iteration over the full time horizon. Their approximation errors come from largely independent sources, so their agreement cross-validates the solution. The PINN-PI scheme also scales more favorably in the state dimension, which points to a route for higher-dimensional extensions.Conceptually, we separate two structurally distinct sources of welfare loss under behavioral biases, an asymmetry channel and a saturation channel, with implications for the design of financial advice and for the literature on household portfolio mistakes. Our work connects with several strands of the broader literature. For portfolio selection under stochastic volatility, Liu (2007) and Chacko and Viceira (2005) provide the foundational treatments of CRRA variance hedging; we depart from their framework by embedding the sen-timent state as a third dimension. From a behavioral household finance perspective, our model offers a unified belief-formation mechanism to rationalize key empirical findings, such as ex-cess trading volume (Barber and Odean, 2000, 2001), the disposition effect (Odean, 1998), and behavioral welfare costs (Cohen et al., 2020). On the numerical front, our solution method syn-thesizes the directional ADI schemes of in ’t Hout and Welfert (2009) and the PINN framework of Raissi et al. (2019). Integrating these two approaches into a Howard policy-iteration loop (Howard, 1960) is, to our knowledge, entirely novel in asset pricing. The remainder of the paper is organized as follows. Section 2 formulates the model and derives the HJB equation. Section 3 states and proves the three main analytical results: the optimal-portfolio decomposition, the semilinear-PDE reduction, and the nesting of Atmaz (2022) as a limiting case. Section 4 analyzes the behavioral anomalies generated by the model. Section 5 reports the numerical results, including parameter values, the cross-validation of the two solution methods, welfare costs, and parameter sensitivity. Section 6 concludes. All proofs are collected 3in Appendix A; algorithmic details of the numerical methods are deferred to Appendix B.

5.2 Numerical Methods 

Because the semilinear PDE (13) lacks a closed-form solution under a non-linear f , we solve it numerically using both ADI and PINN-PI. Structurally, both methods operate within an outer policy-iteration loop. In each step of this loop, a fixed policy linearizes the HJB equation, allowing the respective inner subroutines to solve the linear PDE before the policy is updated via the first-order conditions in Proposition 1. While sharing this conceptual framework, the two methods rely on fundamentally different mathematical foundations, which we deliberately exploit for rigorous cross-validation. The ADI scheme operates on a standard finite-difference paradigm. Time is discretized into Nt explicit backward steps. Within each step, we iterate the policy-allocation and value-evaluation pair to convergence on a 200 × 200 grid in the (v, X ) domain before marching backward. Policy iteration is therefore local in time, meaning that each step maintains a self-contained inner loop where the converged value function at step n supplies the temporal boundary condition for step n − 1.Conversely, the PINN-PI scheme follows a mesh-free paradigm. The value function Ψ( v, X, t )

is approximated by a single neural network parameterized over the entire space-time domain Ω×

[0 , T ]. Policy iteration here is global in time, requiring each outer iteration to retrain the network across the full horizon under the current policy. Automatic differentiation handles all derivatives in the PDE residual, while randomly sampled collocation points replace the structured grid. This structural divergence is intentional because the two methodologies possess largely un-correlated sources of approximation error. The precision of the ADI scheme is restricted by grid spacing and time-step truncation, whereas the error of the PINN-PI scheme depends on net-work expressivity and stochastic optimization. Consequently, mutual agreement between these distinct algorithms provides a robust validation of our underlying solution. Beyond cross-validation, the global-in-time architecture of PINN-PI offers significant scalabil-ity in higher dimensions. While the computational cost of ADI scales exponentially as O(N d ·Nt)

in the number of state variables d, PINN-PI scales only with the network input dimension. This 14 efficiency makes neural networks the natural choice for high-dimensional extensions that incor-porate wealth, multiple risky assets, or additional state dynamics. Although we rely on ADI as our primary solver due to its efficiency in the current two-dimensional state space, the scala-bility of PINN-PI stands as an independent methodological contribution. Appendix B provides the full algorithmic specifications, including discretization stencils, network architectures, and optimization loss functions. # 6 Conclusion 

This paper extends the return-extrapolation framework of Atmaz (2022) to a portfolio-choice setting with two behavioral features that the linear-symmetric benchmark omits: saturation in belief updating (the Weber–Fechner law) and a stronger response to losses than to gains (loss aversion). We characterized the optimal portfolio of a CRRA investor as the sum of a sentiment-distorted myopic demand, a variance hedging demand, and a sentiment hedging demand. We 20 reduced the value-function HJB equation to a semilinear PDE that a finite-difference scheme can solve. We also showed that the linear-extrapolation benchmark of Atmaz (2022) is nested as a limiting case. The model delivers four investor-level predictions: an asymmetric portfolio response to past returns, attenuated marginal trading at extreme sentiment, a positive sentiment-driven channel in trading volume, and a welfare loss that rises with both the extrapolation degree and the asymmetry parameter. Each connects to a documented pattern in the household-finance and behavioral-finance literatures. The numerical analysis adds a less obvious finding: saturation acts as an endogenous correction mechanism . The bounded function f (X) caps the size of belief distortion in extreme states. Across our calibrations, the asymmetric nonlinear extrapolator therefore carries a smaller welfare loss than a linear extrapolator with the same slope at the origin, and the gap widens as the saturation rate rises. This protective role does not appear in the linear-symmetric framework of Atmaz (2022). It follows directly from taking nonlinearity in belief formation seriously. The framework suggests several extensions. First, the finite-difference solution of the semilin-ear PDE (13) allows one to quantify each anomaly, to calibrate the saturation and asymmetry parameters λ and β to survey or experimental data, and to run further sensitivity analyses. Second, the model could be embedded in a heterogeneous-agent equilibrium in which only some investors extrapolate asymmetrically, which would generate endogenous wealth dynamics and new asset-pricing implications. Third, the asymmetric extrapolation channel could be joined with the endogenous volatility-bias mechanism of Atmaz (2022) in a full equilibrium, though the loss of the exponential-affine structure makes this much harder. This appendix provides the algorithmic details of the two numerical methods used in Sec-tion 5. Throughout, we write the value function as Φ( v, X, t ), with the multiplicative separation

B.1 Policy Iteration for the HJB Equation 

Both numerical methods are wrapped in a policy-iteration loop in the spirit of Howard (1960). The original algorithm of Howard (1960) was formulated for discrete-time discrete-state Markov decision processes, where “policy evaluation” solves a linear system for the value function under a fixed policy and “policy improvement” updates the policy via a one-step Bellman maximization. The same conceptual scheme applies to continuous-time HJB equations: freezing the optimal control ω at a candidate ω(k) reduces the nonlinear HJB to a linear parabolic PDE for Φ,which is then solved by an inner subroutine (ADI or PINN in our implementation), and the policy is updated through the first-order condition of Proposition 1. The convergence and consistency of this scheme for HJB-type PDEs is established in the numerical-analysis literature;

A naive equal-weighting (1 , 1, 1) would let the optimizer minimize LPDE at the expense of the other two terms, because the residual loss has many more collocation points than the terminal or boundary losses and so dominates the gradient. The boundary weight wBC = 10 is set at an intermediate level: large enough to pin down ΨX at the sentiment boundaries, but small enough that the Neumann condition does not over-constrain the interior solution at the corners. Two implementation details deserve emphasis. First, log-uniform sampling in v is essential. Table 7 summarizes the structural differences between the two methods. The two methods are therefore not duplicates. They implement the same policy iteration through very different numerical paradigms. We view this as a strength. The agreement reported in Section 5.2 is not a self-consistency check between minor variants of one discretization. It is a cross-validation between two methods whose approximation errors come from largely inde-pendent sources. The time-global structure of PINN-PI also generalizes more readily to higher-dimensional problems, such as those with wealth as a state variable, multiple risky assets, or stochastic interest rates, where the ADI grid scales exponentially in the number of state vari-ables. In the present two-dimensional setting we keep ADI as the primary solver and do not exploit this property; it becomes relevant for the higher-dimensional extensions discussed in Section 6.
