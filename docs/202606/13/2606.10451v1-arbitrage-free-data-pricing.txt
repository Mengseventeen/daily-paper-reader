Abstract 

Driven by the rising value of data in applications such as advertising, finance, and machine learning, markets for data products have become increasingly important. Data markets mainly sell two kinds of products: datasets and machine learning models. Since these products can be replicated at negligible marginal cost, sellers naturally version them through query access and noisy model releases. Versioning immediately raises an arbitrage problem: a buyer may combine cheaper purchases and recover a more informative product at a lower total price. Existing work on query and model pricing studies arbitrage-freeness when buyer values are treated as exogenous, whereas the literature on selling information derives value from the buyer’s decision problem but ignores arbitrage-freeness. Accordingly, we study the seller’s optimal data pricing problem where buyers value data through Bayesian decision making and we impose arbitrage-freeness constraints. We first interpret query and model pricing as special cases of information pricing, and formulate the general arbitrage-free information selling problem, show the computational hardness and give a branch-and-bound algorithm based on McCormick relaxations. We then consider threshold utilities where buyers have a positive value if and only if the experiment is sufficiently informative. Under this condition, we find that the arbitrage-freeness can be characterized by Blackwell dominance, which in turn unifies the arbitrage-free conditions for query pricing [ 14 ] and model pricing [ 11 ]. Finally, we characterize the revenue-maximizing pricing under restricted query and model menus. 

# 1 Introduction 

The growing demand for data in advertising, finance, logistics, and machine learning has given rise to active markets for data products. Two main product forms in data markets are datasets and machine learning models [ 31 ]. On the dataset side, Bloomberg and Xignite sell financial information through terminals, data feeds, and APIs, while AWS Data Exchange, Snowflake Marketplace, and Dawex broker subscriptions and licenses for large catalogs of third-party data products. On the model side, sellers increasingly monetize models rather than raw data itself: AWS SageMaker and Microsoft Azure offer pre-trained models and model training services, while large language model (LLM) companies like OpenAI, Anthropic and Google sell LLM access through token-metered APIs and subscriptions. From an economic perspective, buyers purchase data because it improves their decisions. Consider an advertiser deciding how to allocate a campaign budget across demographic groups, channels, and regions. A dataset describing the attributes and historical behavior of a target population helps the advertiser identify which consumers are likely to respond and therefore

In both cases, the value of the product comes from its effect on a downstream decision problem. This suggests a natural viewpoint: selling data is fundamentally the sale of information that is useful for decision making. Compared with ordinary goods, data has a distinctive cost structure. Producing the first useful unit may require substantial expenditure on collection, cleaning, labeling, and model training, whereas reproducing additional units is often nearly costless. For information goods with this structure, [ 30 ] argue that versioning is an especially effective selling strategy. In dataset markets, a natural form of versioning is query-based access [ 14 , 19 , 20 ]: rather than sell only the full database, the seller offers different queries so that buyers with heterogeneous needs can acquire only the portions of the dataset relevant to their tasks. In model markets, the analogous strategy is to release multiple versions with different noise levels or accuracies [ 11 , 25 ]. Buyers who do not need the best model may still be willing to pay for a cheaper, lower-quality version, whereas they would decline to buy if only the top version were offered. Tiered model and API services, large language model (LLM) services, follow the same logic. Once products are versioned, however, a new problem appears: arbitrage . If the menu is not designed carefully, a buyer may register multiple identities or repeatedly purchase lower-tier products, combine them, and recover the information contained in a higher-tier product at a lower total price. 

Example 1.1 (Query arbitrage) . Consider an advertiser who wants to purchase a table of target users for a marketing campaign. Let the underlying dataset be partitioned into two disjoint subsets D = Dmale ∪ Dfemale , where Dmale and Dfemale contain the male and female target users, respectively. Suppose the seller offers three deterministic query products: 

Qmale (D) = Dmale , Qfemale (D) = Dfemale , Qall (D) = D. 

If the posted prices are p(Qmale ) = 1000 , p (Qfemale ) = 1000 , p (Qall ) = 3000 , then a buyer who wants the full table never purchases Qall directly. By buying Qmale and Qfemale , the buyer recovers Qmale (D) ∪ Qfemale (D) = D = Qall (D) at total cost 2000 < 3000 . Hence the menu admits arbitrage.

Existing work on arbitrage-free pricing for query products [ 10 ] and model products [ 11 , 25 ]typically studies revenue maximization under the assumption that the buyer’s value for each data product is exogenously given. However, in practice the seller only has incomplete information about the buyer’s downstream task, prior information, or payoff function. Moreover, once value is tied to decision making, the arbitrage-free pricing rules studied in previous work need not be sufficient. The two examples just mentioned are cases of exact reconstruction: the cheaper products always yield the expensive one, so arbitrage arises for every buyer preference. But 2the absence of such an exact reconstruction relation does not imply the absence of arbitrage. There may still exist priors and decision problems under which combining products is profitable. Section 4.1 makes this distinction precise. A parallel literature on selling information studies precisely the case in which the buyer’s value comes from a concrete Bayesian decision problem [ 1 , 5 , 9, 12 ]. However, that literature typically does not impose arbitrage constraints across multiple purchased experiments. In Section 2.2, we unify versioned datasets and machine learning models as statistical experiments. Then Example 2.7 shows that if arbitrage constraints are ignored, a menu that is optimal under standard information selling constraints may still admit arbitrage behavior of buyers, thereby reducing the seller’s revenue. 

1.1 Our contributions 

Accordingly, we investigate the seller’s optimal data menu design and pricing problem in a setting where pricing satisfies arbitrage-free constraints and where the value of data is derived from the improvement it brings to the buyer’s decision utility. Specifically, our contributions are as follows: First, we formulate arbitrage-free (AF) constraints for information selling in Section 2. In this formulation, buyers may purchase several products (a bundle), including repeated copies of the same product, and each bundle induces a composite experiment. We prove that incentive compatibility and individual rationality are special cases of AF constraints, and we establish a revelation principle adapted to AF constraints: without loss of generality, one may restrict attention to direct menus that are arbitrage-free against all bundles. Second, in Section 3, we show that repeated purchases can strictly increase informativeness indefinitely, so exact arbitrage-freeness in general induce an infinite set of constraints. We further prove that detecting a profitable bundle deviation is NP-hard even in a highly structured binary signal environment. Motivated by this hardness, we formulate the h-bounded problem as a direct mathematical program, where buyers are only allowed to purchase bundles of size at most h. The resulting program is multilinear because bundle recommendations are products of per-experiment recommendation probabilities. We then give an equivalent tensor lift that replaces these multilinear terms with linear marginal constraints and bilinear minor constraints, yielding a branch-and-bound formulation based on McCormick relaxations. Third, in Section 4.1, we identify threshold utilities as a regime in which arbitrage-freeness becomes much more tractable: for threshold types, the AF constraints are equivalent to a purely informational condition that no cheaper bundle may Blackwell-dominate the assigned experiment for each buyer type in the direct menu. We give explicit constructions of buyer’s decision problems showing that subset-query menus and Gaussian model menus can realize threshold utilities. As a consequence, we recover the arbitrage-free conditions for query and model pricing in previous works as special cases, thus unifying them under the same framework and indicating the arbitrage-free conditions for query and model pricing in previous works are not sufficient for AF in the general setting. Fourth, Section 4.3 studies the revenue-maximization problem once the seller is restricted to some query and model menus under threshold utilities. We first prove that optimal arbitrage-free pricing remains NP-hard for general query menus. We then identify structured cases in which the problem becomes more tractable. For laminar dataset menus, we derive an exact dynamic program with running time O(|F| V 3) when values are integral. For additive-precision model menus, we reduce the problem to monotone subadditive chain pricing in [ 11 ], which implies coNP-hardness of exact optimization and has an O(n2) dynamic program with a 1/2 approximation guarantee. 31.2 Related work 

The literature on data pricing spans many different directions. Here we focus on the two strands most closely related to this paper: arbitrage-free pricing and information selling. For surveys on other topics, such as auction-based pricing, online pricing, privacy pricing, and data valuation, see [7, 13, 28, 31]. 

Arbitrage-free data pricing. The notion of arbitrage-free (AF) data pricing was first introduced by [ 2 ]. [ 20 ] was the first to formalize the arbitrage-free principle in query markets and studied the computational complexity of a special class of arbitrage-free pricing functions. Building on this line of work, [ 22 ] studied arbitrage-free pricing when noise is added to queries. [ 14 , 24 ] considered more general query pricing functions, and in particular [ 14 ] provided necessary and sufficient conditions for arbitrage-free pricing, which are discussed later in Section 2.2. [ 10 ]studied seller revenue guarantees for heuristic arbitrage-free pricing algorithms when buyer query utilities are given. [ 15 , 29 ] investigated the implementation and optimization of arbitrage-free pricing algorithms in large-scale practical database systems. [ 11 ] was the first to introduce the arbitrage-free principle into the pricing of machine learning models; they characterized the necessary and sufficient conditions for arbitrage-free pricing in the model-pricing setting and studied algorithms for maximizing seller revenue given buyers’ utilities over models. [ 25 ] used differential privacy to add noise thus protecting model privacy, extended the model pricing problem studied in [ 11 ], and further discussed the revenue allocation problem among data providers based on the Shapley value. Our work unifies query and model pricing within the more general problem of pricing information. We show that the arbitrage-free conditions proposed in these prior works are special cases of Blackwell arbitrage-freeness, and therefore are not sufficient in the most general Bayesian decision-theoretic setting. 

Selling information. The theoretical foundations of selling information lie in the study of the value of information [ 17 ] and information design [ 16 , 18 ]. Surveys of the literature on selling information can be found in [ 3 , 4 ]. [ 1] was the first to study the sale of information with multiple rounds of interaction between buyer and seller. They focused on revelation principles under both independent and correlated buyer-seller information structures, namely, reducing information selling mechanisms to simple generic interactive protocols, and they also studied the associated computational complexity. [ 12 ] further incorporated budget constraints and interpreted optimal information selling mechanisms as practical consulting mechanisms. [ 26 ] showed that, in a special class of decision problems and buyer utility specifications, the optimal mechanism for selling information takes the form of a natural threshold mechanism. [ 5] studied the menu-based sale of information, in which buyer and seller interact only once, and characterized the optimal menu when the state space is binary. [ 9 ] studied the corresponding optimal information pricing problem from an algorithmic perspective. [ 6] examined the approximate optimality of selling full information when the binary state and binary action assumptions in [ 5 ] are extended to ternary ones. [ 23 ] studied mechanisms for selling information when data buyers possess endogenous information. Our work builds on the menu-based model of selling information in [ 5 ] and introduces arbitrage-free constraints into the information pricing problem, which is a non-trivial generaliza-tion of previous works. 42 Preliminaries

Remark 2.4 (Relation to [ 1, 5, 9 , 12 ]) . Our model is closely related to several standard formulations in the literature on selling information. As in [ 5, 9], we study the sale of a menu of experiments. The difference is that in their model all buyer types share the same utility function, that is, uθ(ω, a ) = u(ω, a ), ∀θ ∈ Θ, whereas our model allows the utility function to depend on the buyer’s type. The works [ 1, 12 ] study generic interactive protocols for selling information. Such protocols are more general than experiment menus: they may sell either an experiment ex ante or a realized signal ex post. When all buyer types have the same prior, namely μθ = μ, ∀θ ∈ Θ, the optimal generic interactive protocol reduces to a menu of experiments, so our model captures that case as well. In contrast, when buyers have heterogeneous priors, the optimal generic interactive protocol need not be representable as a menu of experiments. Extending our results to this more general setting is an interesting direction for future work.

# 3 General Arbitrage-free Information Selling 

In this section, we will give a formal formulation of the general AF information selling problem based on equation (1) . We first show that finding an arbitraging bundle is computationally hard. Motivated by this hardness, we restrict attention to bundles of size at most a constant h. We formulate the resulting h-bounded problem as a mathematical program and show that it is a multilinear optimization problem, which makes it hard to solve. We then rewrite the program in a form that can be solved by a branch-and-bound algorithm.

# 5 Conclusion and Future Work 

We study the seller’s optimal data pricing problem when the value of data is derived from its effect on the buyer’s decision utility and when buyers may arbitrage by combining multiple purchases. Our framework models versioned datasets and noisy machine-learning models in a common experiment-based language, establishes direct-menu reductions for bundle-robust design, and formalizes the general arbitrage-free problem. We show that the general problem is computationally hard and admits a multilinear h-bounded formulation with a tensor-lifted branch-and-bound implementation. We then identify threshold utilities as a regime in which the full arbitrage-free constraints collapse to Blackwell arbitrage-free, thereby recovering the standard query and model pricing conditions as special cases. Finally, under menu restrictions and threshold utilities, we characterize a computational frontier: general deterministic set menus remain hard, laminar dataset menus admit exact dynamic programming, and additive-precision model menus reduce to monotone subadditive chain pricing with known hardness and approximation guarantees. Several directions remain for future work. First, as noted in Section 2, it would be natural to extend the analysis from experiment menus to more general interactive information selling mechanisms. Second, as noted in Section 3.3, the h-bounded branch-and-bound formulation provides an exact benchmark but may be computationally expensive in the worst case, so designing faster algorithms and carrying out a systematic empirical evaluation are important next steps. Third, it would be useful to develop practical pricing algorithms for other realistic market environments, such as overlapping data subsets, or LLM services with multiple quality, latency, context-window, or safety tiers. 17 References
