# Causal Separation, Conditional Risk, and Projected Markowitz Portfolios 

Alejandro Rodr´ ıguez Dom´ ınguez ∗

July 7, 2026 

Abstract 

We formalize a single structural condition on a portfolio problem, causal separation: conditional on the realized path of a declared set of drivers through the investment horizon, asset returns are mutually independent. From this condition we derive the complete static portfolio theory it induces. Separation forces a diagonal-plus-low-rank conditional covariance through an exact tower decomposition, with the low-rank block identified as the response to driver innovations, and the constrained mean–variance problem admits a closed-form projected Markowitz solution in which the classical information matrix is replaced by its projection onto the constraint-compatible subspace. We prove uniqueness of the minimal sufficient separator as an information set; invariance of all derived objects under separator equivalence and reparametrization of the driver state; a conditional efficient-frontier theorem; a Hansen– Jagannathan bound whose gap to the unconstrained bound is an exact quadratic form in the shadow prices of the constraint geometry; a conditioning bound showing that separation regularizes estimation through the idiosyncratic variance floor; an exact two-stage solver whose cost is linear in the number of assets; and exact first-order sensitivity bounds under approximate separation at a certified tolerance. The causal content of the framework is stated and proved rather than assumed: under an explicit structural margin the common causes form a separator, observational data identify their realized information and nothing more, causal and correlational separators of equal fit are distinguished by interventions on non-parents, and an unexplained common cause leaves a quantifiable floor on the dependence diagnostic, so latent confounding is detected rather than absorbed. Seven reproducible experiments validate the theory, with five structural identities holding at machine precision, and quantify its practical content: out-of-sample estimation risk against sample, shrinkage and principal-component covariances, robustness under injected residual dependence, invariance under an intervention that collapses a correlational alternative, and computational scaling to thousands of assets. 

# 1 Introduction 

Mean–variance portfolio choice [ 15 ] is exactly solvable and notoriously fragile: the plug-in solution amplifies estimation error in the mean and in the inverse covariance, to the point that equal weighting is hard to beat out of sample [ 17 , 1 , 4 , 12 ]. The standard responses regularize the covariance, through shrinkage [ 14 ], norm constraints [ 9 ], or factor structure [ 3, 6 ], and remain agnostic about a prior question: conditional on what information should the moments be computed? This paper takes the selection question as primitive and answers it with a single structural condition imported from the theory of common causes [ 24 , 21 ]. Fix a declared, finite universe of candidate drivers, and call a subset a separator if, conditional on its realized path through the investment horizon, the asset returns are mutually independent. The condition is Reichenbach’s screening-off property: whatever co-moves the assets is attributable to the conditioning set, so that what remains is idiosyncratic by construction. Everything in the paper is derived from this one condition and two regularity assumptions. The contributions are fivefold. First, we formalize causal separation at the level of σ-algebras, with a conditioning window that closes at the investment horizon and a companion requirement that the asset past is itself screened, and we develop the associated selection theory: the optimal approximate separator exists by construction over the declared universe, the minimal sufficient separator is unique as an information set, and every derived object is invariant under separator equivalence and under smooth reparametrization of the driver state, so the theory depends on the information and not on its coordinates. Second, we make the causal semantics of the condition explicit and prove exactly what it does and does not claim: under a stated structural margin the common causes form a separator; observational data identify the realized information of the causes and not the causal labels; causal and correlational separators of equal observational fit are distinguished by interventions on non-parents; and an unexplained common cause leaves a quantifiable floor on the dependence functional, so latent confounding is detected rather than silently absorbed. Third, we derive the exact structure of conditional risk that the condition induces: the return vector factorizes conditionally, an exact tower decomposition over the two conditioning windows yields a diagonal-plus-low-rank covariance at the decision node, and the low-rank block is identified as the matrix of responses to driver innovations, so the decomposition is a theorem rather than a modelling choice. Fourth, we solve the constrained mean–variance problem in the induced geometry in closed form: the solution is classical Markowitz with the information matrix replaced by its projection onto the constraint-compatible subspace, two-fund separation survives, the conditional efficient frontier is a parabola with an explicit potential, the maximal conditional Sharpe ratio obeys a Hansen–Jagannathan bound whose gap to the unconstrained bound is an exact quadratic form in the shadow prices of the constraint geometry, the entire problem is conditioned by the idiosyncratic variance floor, and an exact two-stage solver with cost linear in the number of assets follows from the structure. Fifth, we develop an exact sensitivity theory under approximate separation, with first-order formulas and distribution-free worst-case bounds for the displacement of the solution and of the frontier at a given tolerance, and we validate every claim in a reproducible suite of seven experiments covering structural identities at machine precision, the two-window factorization, the realized conditional frontier, out-of-sample estimation risk, robustness under injected dependence, invariance under an intervention, and computational scaling. The scope is deliberately static and mathematical. Dynamics, in which the separator and the response maps move and the intertemporal problem acquires hedging demands in the sense of [ 16 ], the finite-sample statistical certification of separators in the sense of [ 29 ], and the equilibrium consequences of widespread adoption of the resulting optimizers all lie beyond the present paper. No claim is made about real markets: whether good separators exist for liquid universes, at what tolerance and with what survival times, is an empirical question that the mathematics here makes precise but cannot settle. What the paper establishes is the exact content of the separation condition for static portfolio choice and its measurable consequences in controlled environments. 2The remainder of the paper is organized as follows. Section 2 introduces the separation condition, its two conditioning windows, the selection theory of optimal separators, and the causal semantics of the framework, with its soundness, identification and interventional-invariance results. Section 3 derives the conditional factorization, the tower decomposition and the induced covariance structure, together with the geometry of exposures and admissibility. Section 4 develops the projected Markowitz theory, and Section 5 the sensitivity theory under approximate separation. Section 6 reports the seven experiments, and Section 7 concludes. The complete experiment configuration is collected in Section A. 

1.1 Related literature 

The fragility of plug-in mean–variance portfolios is documented and quantified in [ 17 , 1, 12 ,4 ], and the robust-optimization response to parameter and model uncertainty is developed in [ 10 ]. The covariance-regularization literature addresses the same fragility through shrinkage [ 14 ], through norm constraints whose implicit shrinkage explains their effectiveness [ 9 ], and through factor structure, from the arbitrage-pricing foundations of approximate factor models [ 3 ] to high-dimensional factor-based covariance estimation [ 6 ]. The role of conditioning information in asset pricing is classical [ 8 , 7 ], and the conditional factor literature makes the second moment conditional through instrumented principal components [ 13 ] and dynamic conditional correlations [ 5 ]. In all of these approaches the conditioning information, or the factor space, is an input: none of them selects the conditioning set, tests it, or attaches invariance guarantees to it, and these are the questions the present paper takes as primitive. On the causal side, the screening-off property of common causes originates with [ 24 ] and is developed in the graphical and structural literature [ 20 , 21 , 23 ]. Invariant causal prediction [ 22 ]identifies causal predictors by their stability under interventions, the principle our intervention experiment instantiates in portfolio space; the hardness of conditional-independence testing and the generalised covariance measure are established in [ 29 ], and the econometrics of generated regressors in two-stage estimation goes back to [ 19 ]. In finance, [ 18 ] argues on methodological grounds that factor investing should be grounded in causal rather than associational structure; the present paper can be read as supplying portfolio-level mathematics for that agenda. The closest line of work is the causal, sensitivity-based portfolio optimization framework of Rodr´ ıguez Dom´ ınguez. In that framework, drivers of asset and portfolio dynamics are defined and optimally selected through the commonality principle, which identifies the common causal drivers of the constituents as the optimal drivers of the portfolio’s diversification dynamics, with the argument resting on Reichenbach’s common cause principle; portfolio constituents are embedded in the space of their sensitivities to the common drivers, where hierarchical and geometric methods deliver idiosyncratic and systematic diversification, with asset dynamics modelled by partial differential equations and sensitivities estimated by neural networks and automatic differentiation [ 25 ]. The framework is developed in [ 27 ] into a general theory of causal portfolio optimization, including the geometry of the common causal manifold, projection methods that preserve idiosyncratic diversification while optimizing systematic diversification in the sensitivity space, and combinatorial methodologies for causal driver selection. Its dynamic side comprises a conditional risk-neutral partial differential equation for the portfolio law given its common causal drivers, supporting dynamic risk management through implied conditional volatilities and weights [ 26 ], and, in full generality, causal PDE-control models that couple forward density evolution, backward stochastic control and nonlinear filtering under partial information, grounded in causal invariance principles [ 28 ]. The present paper is complementary to this framework and narrower in scope: it isolates the 3probabilistic condition that underlies conditioning on common causal drivers, takes that condition as the primitive at the level of σ-algebras, and characterizes what it alone delivers for static mean–variance choice, with selection, uniqueness, invariance and robustness guarantees. The sensitivity-based and PDE-control formulations operate in dynamic, generally nonlinear settings under partial information; the static theory developed here is an analysis of the conditioning condition itself, prior to those layers. A reader will recognize the induced diagonal-plus-low-rank covariance as that of an approximate factor model, and the relation to that literature is best stated as three nested readings, each with its own assumptions. When the factor space is known, the method reduces to structured factor-covariance Markowitz with linear constraints, and in a static linear world the projected solution numerically coincides with the factor-model solution; our experiments confirm the coincidence, and we regard it as evidence that the framework has the correct classical limit. When the driver space is selected rather than given, the contribution is the selection criterion itself, with existence, uniqueness, minimality and invariance guarantees, together with the testability of screening-off as a conditional-independence property of an identified information set rather than an unidentified latent rotation. The causal reading, finally, requires additional structural assumptions or interventional evidence, and the paper states exactly which: under an explicit structural margin the common causes form a separator, observational data identify their realized information and no more, and causal and correlational separators of equal fit are distinguished by interventions on non-parents, a property our experiments measure directly. Nothing in the second reading depends on the third, and nothing in the portfolio theory depends on either.

2.4 Causal semantics: soundness, identification, and interventional invariance 

The separation condition of Theorem 2.4 is stated, and is testable, at the purely observational level: it is a conditional-independence property of an identified σ-algebra, and every portfolio-level result of Sections 3 to 5 is a consequence of that property alone. The adjective causal is nevertheless earned, in a precise and limited sense that this subsection makes explicit through three statements: a soundness theorem (causal structure implies separation), an identification proposition (what observational data can and cannot identify), and an interventional invariance theorem (the property that distinguishes a causal separator from an observationally equivalent correlational one, and the one our intervention experiment measures). A fourth statement shows that an unexplained common cause leaves a quantifiable floor on the dependence functional, so that latent confounding is detected rather than silently absorbed. 

Assumption 2.16 (A4: structural margin) . There is a structural causal model over the window 

[t − δ, t + H] whose variables include the driver values {Dks }, the returns {ris}, and mutually independent exogenous noises, such that: (i) the joint law is Markov with respect to the associated acyclic graph; (ii) returns are sink nodes: within the window, no return is a cause of any other return, of any driver, or of any other variable (no contagion channel among assets and no feedback from returns to drivers at the horizon of the decision); (iii) the exogenous noise entering each return is specific to that asset and date; (iv) every variable that is a common cause of two or more assets’ returns within the window has its window path measurable with respect to FC 

> [t−δ,t +h]

for a subset C ⊆ { 1, . . . , M } of the declared universe; (v) every variable in C is an ancestor of the returns or exogenous: no declared driver in C is a common effect of variables outside C.

Part (ii) is the substantive economic content: it excludes direct cross-asset causation (for example, fire-sale contagion operating inside the horizon) except insofar as such channels are themselves represented by declared drivers; part (iv) says the universe is rich enough to observe the common causes; part (v) excludes collider conditioning, and its practical reading is a warning: 8an index constructed by aggregating asset-specific causes, and in particular any driver derived from the assets themselves, is a dangerous conditioner, since conditioning on a common effect manufactures dependence between its causes. Neither faithfulness nor linearity is assumed.

Remark 5.3 (From tolerance to confidence) . Theorems 5.1 and 5.2 take ϵ as an input. Turning the estimated ˆϵt into a finite-sample confidence statement requires conditional-independence test batteries, post-selection error control over the candidate lattice, and sequentially valid monitoring; these are statistical developments beyond the scope of this paper, and any such development converts a statistical tolerance into portfolio risk through exactly the two bounds above. The two instruments of this section have deliberately different jobs: the exact first-order formula of Theorem 5.1 is the working diagnostic, sharp on the realized pattern, while the distribution-free bound of Theorem 5.2 is a certificate, uniform over every pattern consistent with the tolerance and correspondingly conservative; the conservatism is quantified in the experiments and is the price of uniformity, not an inefficiency of the analysis. 5.1 Failure modes and the domain of the assumptions 

The assumptions are strong and their failure modes are part of the theory’s honest perimeter. The idiosyncratic floor (A3)(iii) enters every constant: the conditioning bound, the Lipschitz constants and the worst-case sensitivity all scale as ς−2, so a small floor degrades the guarantees quantitatively and visibly rather than invalidating them; an asset with negligible idiosyncratic variance is, in this framework, an asset whose risk is entirely systematic, and the bounds say precisely that such an asset makes the projected problem as ill-conditioned as the classical one. Near collinearity of driver innovations makes Λ nearly singular; the solution and frontier remain well defined as long as Q ≻ 0, which the floor guarantees, but the identification of individual columns of B degrades, and the two-stage solver should then use a factored or pseudoinverse form of Λ; the gauge invariance results are the formal statement that only the column space, not the coordinates, carries meaning. Heteroskedasticity is accommodated by construction: the diagonal block of the covariance is E[Σ ct | G t], which may depend on the state, and the floor is required only as a uniform lower bound; conditional-variance dynamics affect the power of correlation-based tests, for which the generalised covariance measure [ 29 ] is the appropriate instrument. Heavy tails split into two separate questions: the separation property is a statement about conditional laws and survives without moments, while the mean–variance layer requires conditional second moments, and correlation-based dependence functionals lose validity, so rank-based or kernel-based functionals should replace them in the definition of ϵt, which Theorem 2.7 permits. Conditional independence throughout means independence of conditional laws; the 16 working dependence functional operationalizes it within a declared test class, and every certificate is relative to that class, a relativity that Theorem 2.7 makes explicit rather than implicit. Finally, the intersection property (A2) fails under exact deterministic redundancy among drivers, for instance a duplicated driver, in which case minimal sufficient separators need not be unique as subsets; they remain equivalent as σ-algebras, which is the invariance the theory actually uses, and the failure is detectable as exact collinearity in the fitted driver dynamics.

6.2 E1: the two windows are empirically sharp 

Figure 1 conditions the same simulated panel ( n = 30, m = 3, T = 500, 400 replications) two ways. Past-window residuals (returns regressed on Zt alone) carry the full systematic block BΛB⊤: the maximal absolute off-diagonal residual correlation is 0 .83, and Fisher-z tests reject independence for 82% of pairs at the 5% level. Closed-window residuals (regressed on ( Zt, ˆηt+1 )) show no dependence: maximal off-diagonal correlation 0 .025 on a T = 20 ,000 path, and the rejection rate tracks the nominal level along the 45 ◦ line, with size 4 .5% at the 5% level. These results are the empirical counterpart of Theorem 2.6: separation holds exactly where the theory places it, on the horizon-closed algebra, and fails exactly where the theory forbids it, on the past algebra, while every regressor used by the tests is past data at test time. One econometric caveat applies: the conditioning regressors include the estimated innovations ˆη, so the Fisher-z battery operates on generated regressors [ 19 ] and its degrees-of-freedom adjustment is not the generated-regressor correction. The observed size indicates that the distortion is negligible in this design, and the generalised covariance measure of [ 29 ], constructed to tolerate first-stage estimation at o(T −1/4)rates, is a direct replacement where formal guarantees are required. 

6.3 E2: estimation risk out of sample 

Table 1 reports the estimation-risk comparison. Global-minimum-variance portfolios are built from seven covariance estimators, namely the sample covariance, Ledoit–Wolf shrinkage [ 14 ], principal components with the factor count misspecified low ( m − 2), correct ( m) and misspecified high (m+2), the causal structured ˆQ, and the oracle Q, across n ∈ { 50 , 100 , 200 } and T ∈ { 126 , 252 , 504 },18 with 250 Monte Carlo replications per cell, evaluated on 252 out-of-sample days. Monte Carlo standard errors are below 0 .06 percentage points of volatility, excluding the singular cell, and 0 .17 of Sharpe. Two designs are reported for the mean–variance rows. Under the static-hold design, weights are computed once, at the signal ZT , and held for the entire out-of-sample year; this is the one-shot deployment that isolates estimation risk from signal dynamics, and since the signal half-life is approximately fourteen days, static Sharpe levels are intentionally modest. Under the daily-rebalanced design, fitted parameters are frozen and weights are recomputed each day at the current state. Because Sharpe levels in either design are artifacts of the simulator’s premium scale, Table 2 reports a scale-free quantity: the fraction of the same-policy oracle Sharpe ratio captured, with static estimators normalized by the static oracle, whose level is a realistic 1 .1 annualized, and the rebalanced strategy normalized by the rebalanced oracle, whose level of 11 .8 at this daily signal strength is a simulator artifact that the ratio removes. The table also reports the mean daily one-way turnover of the rebalanced causal strategy as a fraction of gross exposure. Four observations follow. First, the sample covariance degrades as n/T grows and fails at 

n = 200, T = 126, where realized volatility reaches 277%: the training covariance is rank deficient, the associated KKT system is numerically singular, and the solver returns the unstable weights that constitute the documented pathology, the canonical Markowitz failure [ 17 , 1, 12 ]. Second, the structured estimator matches the oracle to a few basis points of annualized volatility at every (n, T ), and its calibration remains at 1 .03 to 1 .04 where the sample covariance reaches 26 and shrinkage 2 .5; predicted risk is therefore reliable, which is Theorem 4.5 in operation, and Fig. 2 displays both metrics across n. Third, the classical-limit agreement discussed in Section 1.1 has a converse: principal components with the correct factor count are indistinguishable from the causal estimator, but principal components missing two factors carry 46% more realized risk, 2 .13% against 1 .46% at n = 200, T = 252, while their calibration of 1 .10 understates the misspecification. This failure mode of latent-factor selection is removed by an identified conditioning set, since the causal estimator does not choose a factor count: the drivers are declared observables. Fourth, regarding the mean, the sample-mean plug-in captures between 0 and 18% of the attainable static Sharpe ratio, against 39 to 76% for the conditional mean on the selected state, rising with T in both designs. Turnover of the rebalanced strategy is a steady 29% of gross exposure per day at this signal speed, a magnitude at which transaction costs are first order, so no cost-free performance claim is made and the friction-aware treatment is left to future work.

6.9 From simulation to field data: a pre-specified evaluation protocol 

The experiments above are synthetic by design, because the paper’s claims are mathematical and controlled environments are where mathematical claims are falsifiable. The empirical question they do not and cannot answer, whether good approximate separators exist in actual markets, deserves a pre-specified protocol rather than an afterthought, and the reproducibility package implements one in full ( field protocol.py ), runnable on any user-supplied panel of returns and candidate drivers. The protocol fixes, before any data are seen: the declared universes, for example equity panels with candidate drivers drawn from traded factor portfolios, sector indices, volatility indices, rates and commodities; currency panels with rates, carry, a dollar factor and volatility; or futures panels with inflation, rates and trend variables; the windowing, with selection and certification on disjoint segments of each rolling window and evaluation strictly out of sample, following the 24 workflow of Section 6.1; and the reported quantities, namely the certified tolerance ˆϵt per window with its time series and stability across windows, the selected sets and their survival times, and the same functional evaluated on the residuals of principal-component factor models with matched dimension as the reference. The protocol’s outcomes are informative in every direction: small and stable certified tolerances would locate universes where the theory’s premise holds, large ones are, by Theorem 2.20, evidence of unobserved common causes and therefore a measurement of what is missing from the declared universe rather than a failure of the method, and instability quantifies the survival-time question directly. No performance claim is attached to the protocol; its object is the certificate, which is the quantity this paper’s theory prices. 

# 7 Discussion 

The paper isolates what a single structural condition delivers in static portfolio choice. The condition, screening-off by a declared driver set with the horizon-closed conditioning window that Theorem 2.6 shows to be forced, yields the diagonal-plus-low-rank conditional covariance as a theorem, the projected Markowitz solution in closed form, a realized conditional frontier, an exact price for the admissibility geometry, regularization by the idiosyncratic floor, invariance under both separator equivalence and reparametrization, an O(nk 2) exact solver, and computable sensitivity to the certified tolerance ϵ. The experiments confirm each class of claim at the appropriate resolution: identities at machine precision, statistical properties at nominal levels, and economic magnitudes in realistic ranges. Three developments are deliberately excluded and remain open. The first is dynamics: when the separator, the response maps and the admissible geometry move, the state variable of the intertemporal problem is the manifold itself, and the optimal policy adds to (13) a hedging demand against manifold motion in the sense of [ 16 ]. The second is certification: the finite-sample statistics of the estimated tolerance, including test batteries across model classes, error control after selection over the candidate lattice and sequential monitoring, together with a transfer theorem converting a statistical tolerance into the risk bounds of Section 5. The third is equilibrium: the consequences of adoption of the optimizers of Theorem 4.1 by a continuum of agents whose trading feeds back into the drivers. Two limitations should be stated. The experiments are synthetic by design: the paper’s claims are mathematical, and controlled environments are where mathematical claims are testable; whether good separators exist for liquid universes, at what tolerance and with what survival times, is an empirical question rather than a corollary of the theory, and Section 6.9 specifies, and the package implements, the protocol by which it should be answered. The synthetic choice here is deliberate and complementary to the empirical record of the framework it analyzes: the sensitivity-based and PDE-control line of [ 25 , 26 , 27 , 28 ] reports extensive implementations on real asset panels and real driver universes, including equity and multi-asset allocations and brokerage-data studies, where the object of interest is out-of-sample portfolio performance. The present paper sets performance aside precisely in order to isolate theoretical validation: in a controlled environment the structural identities, the factorization, the frontier and the invariance claims can be checked exactly and falsifiably against a known data-generating process, which real data cannot provide. The two modes are complementary, empirical evidence of value on one side and exact verification of the mathematics on the other, and the field protocol connects them by carrying the theoretical object, the certified tolerance, to real panels. The conditional mean, moreover, remains as difficult an input as it has always been [ 12 , 4 ]: the framework locates the mean on a low-dimensional selected state and prices the consequences of its errors, but it does not manufacture predictability that is 25 absent. What it changes is the question asked of the data, from the identity of the covariance to the identity of the conditioning set that renders risk idiosyncratic, and everything in this paper follows from taking that question and its answer as the primitive of portfolio construction.
